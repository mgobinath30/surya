Photogram

An AR experience for memory exploration and photography.

Problem

Photography in it's current medium has limitations.

Distance

2D photography has three problems that are deeply integrated into the fabric of society.

  1. Distance from memories: 2D photography is limited. A picture doesn’t fully represent a memory because a memory is dynamic. Environment, movement, feeling, and changing emotions, are all key aspects of a memory that a picture can’t capture.
  2. Distance from the truth: Fake news! Social media and 2D photos obscure reality. We must look towards more accurate methods of capturing moments in time in order to have an accurate understanding for the world around us.
  3. Distance from the friends: Photoshop and related tools further create inaccurate representations of the world around us.

Location

Currently, media isn’t associated to the location that it’s created in. Sure, we can see a pin on a map for where we took a picture on a smartphone, but we need to fully feel the environment to get a real understanding of the media’s context.

Solution

Photogram is the photography app of the future. Photogram allows people to relive their memories in the locations where they happened. By seamlessly combining 3D scanning, audio, video, and location, Photogram captures your environment with the Microsoft HoloLens for playback, bringing the digital world one step closer to the physical world.


How do we represent memories?

We started with drawings, then pictures, audio, video, and now Photogram. Photogram represents the next step in this progression. It answers the question: What will memories look like in mixed reality? Does this medium bring us one step closer to their memories?

AR Log Line

"A participant puts on the Hololens and has the ability to capture a memory in a 3D space (a “Photogram”), where they are able to play back their own past Photograms, connect with the Photograms of others, and explore the Photograms of that location. The experience goal is to redefine memory retention, recall, and development while capitalizing on empathy."

Interaction Inspiration

When researching and testing interaction techniques, we drew inspiration from camera mode dials, hollywood clapperboards, and photogrammetry methods. It’s important to incorporate familiar icons and and interactions when creating an entirely new way of looking at something that people do every day, in this case, photography. Doing so makes it easier for people to understand and adopt the product. For example, when the iPhone launched, the icons used for the phone, contacts, and mail were 2D representations of the products people already knew. It doesn’t matter that a land line phone isn’t the most common way to call people, having it as the icon for a phone made it easier for people to integrate the iPhone into their daily lifestyle. As such, we opted for a camera dial interaction as are our menu.

Before organizing and placing the various menu within the app, we started broad and created a multitude of icons with different images. Our first step was to figure out which icons represented their intended feature best through user research, surveying, and testing.

Icons: 3D

As we started to prototype the camera dial for our menu, we realized that it didn't make sense for 2D icons to be overlayed onto a 3D dial. Despite traditional camera dials following this pattern, the 3D nature of an AR environment enabled us to make our icons more dynamic and engaging. As such, we moved in the direction of using 3D icons.
